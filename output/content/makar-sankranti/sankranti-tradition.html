How to Celebrate Sankranti

Sankranti is a seasonal turning point celebrated with gratitude, food, worship, charity, and community. Across India, the same mid‑January window is marked in different ways—Makar Sankranti (north & central India), Uttarayan (Gujarat), Pongal (Tamil Nadu), and Magh/Bhogali Bihu (Assam). This page brings the traditions together in one practical guide—highlighting unique, old customs that are still cherished.

Quick start: 10 traditional ways to observe Sankranti

This portion updates the foundational advice from the initial page (including daily practices, Surya reverence, acts of kindness, til sweets, khichdi, and visits with family) while incorporating a local perspective.

1) Begin with Surya gratitude

Try to wake up early and give water (arghya) to the rising sun, while praying for clarity, health, and positive energy. Carry this intention with you throughout the day.

2) Mantra, meditation, or quiet reflection

Some families choose to chant Gayatri or recite prayers, while others prefer meditation, viewing Sankranti as a time to reset for the year ahead.

3) Remember ancestors (tarpan)

Certain customs involve making water offerings to ancestors and elders as a way to show appreciation for values, education, and the preservation of family ties.

4) Give daan (donation)

Charity plays a vital role in providing food, clothing, sesame/jaggery, blankets during winter, and helping temples and community kitchens.

5) Prepare til sweets

Prepare til-gur sweets such as tilgul, laddoo, and chikki to offer at a shrine or temple, and share them as prasad to symbolize sweetness in communication

6) Cook a simple harvest meal

In many areas, a simple meal of Khichdi (a mix of rice and dal) with seasonal vegetables is a symbol of simplicity, nourishment, and thankfulness.

7) Visit family and elders

Traditional practices prioritize spending time with children and elders, exchanging gifts to foster family connections and uphold cultural values.

8) Celebrate outdoors

In numerous locations, individuals gather on rooftops or in open fields to enjoy activities like kite flying, fairs, sports, and community events beneath the winter sun.

9) Holy dip (where applicable)

Certain communities partake in a sacred bath either in rivers or at pilgrimage sites, representing purification and the setting of new intentions.

10) Begin something auspicious

Sankranti is often considered an auspicious day for embarking on new endeavors such as learning, getting engaged or married, or beginning a new routine.

Old tradition, modern meaning

These traditions blend spiritual beliefs with societal responsibilities through acts of worship, thanksgiving, communal meals, and charitable giving to support the community during the colder months.

Regional celebrations: what makes each unique

Sankranti season is like a single “festival window” with multiple cultural expressions. Here’s how the best-known traditions differ, including unique older customs that remain visible today.

Makar Sankranti (Makara Sankranti)

Core idea

The sun's shift and the season of giving thanks; a period for generosity, reverence, and communal sharing.

Old traditions

  • Til-gur sweets exchanged to promote sweet speech and unity
  • Khichdi and seasonal foods as “simple living” symbolism
  • Visits to elders/children with gifts and blessings
  • Daan of food and winter essentials

In numerous households, Surya worship is paired with sharing sweets, hosting guests, and contributing to community causes, while in certain areas, festivities and river ceremonies are integral to the celebrations.

Uttarayan (Gujarat)

Signature tradition

Flying kites on rooftops turns neighborhoods into vibrant festival grounds, filled with music, delicious food, and friendly competition.

Old customs

  • Community rooftop gatherings (open houses for neighbors)
  • Kite craft traditions (paper, bamboo, thread techniques)
  • Seasonal foods carried to rooftops for sharing
  • Music and collective chanting during peak kite hours

During Uttarayan, the sky becomes the festival venue, where we celebrate the radiant winter sun and the communal happiness of gathering outdoors.

Pongal (Tamil Nadu)

Four-day festival

Bhogi, Surya Pongal, Mattu Pongal, and Kaanum Pongal involve getting ready, worshipping, honoring cattle, and visiting

Unique & old traditions

  • Boiling-over Pongal pot ritual (symbol of abundance)
  • Kolam (rice-flour designs) renewed daily
  • Sugarcane as a signature harvest symbol
  • Mattu Pongal: bathing, decorating, honoring cattle

Pongal is rooted in agriculture, expressing appreciation for the sun, nature, and the animals and individuals that sustain the farming community.

Magh / Bhogali Bihu (Assam)

What stands out

A celebration of abundance post-harvest: Uruka feast and Meji bonfire at sunrise.

Unique & old traditions

  • Bhelaghar huts built from bamboo and leaves
  • Meji bonfire worship at dawn for blessings
  • Community feasts that strengthen village ties
  • Traditional games and local sports in some areas

Magh Bihu showcases traditional harvest elements like plentiful food, communal warmth, and a strong sense of community, particularly prevalent in rural Assam.

Shared thread

In every area, the ancient essence endures: thankfulness for crops and sunshine, communal feasts, and strengthening ties within the community.
