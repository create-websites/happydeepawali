Dussehra's Hidden Hues: Lesser-Known Local & Tribal Rituals

Amidst the towering effigies of Ravana and lively Ramlila enactments, India celebrates Dussehra with a mosaic of distinct traditions. In secluded villages and tribal regions, the festivities come alive through age-old rituals, nature veneration, and devotion to regional deities. These hidden customs reveal the rich cultural diversity woven across the nation.

Bastar's 75-Day Spectacle

In Chhattisgarh's tribal heartland, Bastar Dussehra spans an epic 75 days, honoring the local deity, Danteshwari. Unlinked to the Ramayana, it blends tribal, Hindu, and royal customs in colorful rituals like:

  • Pat Jatra: The ritual honoring of timber for building a grand chariot.
  • Kachan Gadi: A young girl, thought to channel the spirit of Kachan Devi, initiates the festivities by swaying on a thorn-laden cradle.
  • Muria Durbar: A gathering of tribal leaders convened to address key community issues alongside the administration.

Kullu's Divine Assembly

Set amid the scenic valleys of Himachal Pradesh, Kullu Dussehra is a vibrant week-long global festival. It kicks off on Vijayadashami, the day festivities end elsewhere in India. A key attraction is the majestic procession of countless local deities, borne on richly adorned palanquins, in honor of Lord Raghunath (Rama).

  • Rath Yatra: Devotees from across the valley pull the chariot of Lord Raghunath.
  • Lanka Dahan: On the final day, a stack of wood and straw is ignited by the Beas river, representing the fiery end of Lanka.

Kulasekarapattinam's Trance and Fire

The coastal town of Kulasekarapattinam in Tamil Nadu hosts a distinctive Dussehra celebration at the Mutharamman Temple. Devotees, often in a trance-like state, embody various deities, with many channeling the fierce goddess Kali. The event features lively processions, traditional music, and fire-walking, filling the air with energy and devotion.

Worship of Ravana

Breaking from the usual tradition, some Indian communities honor Ravana during Dussehra. In Mandsaur, Madhya Pradesh, he is celebrated as a wise scholar and the city's son-in-law, as Mandodari, his wife, is said to be from there. Similarly, Maharashtra's Gond tribe venerates Ravana, seeing him as a figure of intellect and strength.

The Significance of the Shami Tree

Across rural India, the Shami tree is deeply revered during Dussehra. Legend says the Pandavas concealed their weapons in it during exile. On Vijayadashami, people honor the tree and share its leaves, called 'sona' (gold), as a token of luck and prosperity.

Madikeri's Grand Carnival

In Karnataka's Coorg hills, Madikeri Dasara is a century-old spectacle. The festival features a dazzling procession of massive, illuminated tableaus portraying mythological tales. Crafted over months by temples and local teams, these tableaus vie fiercely for the prestigious top honor.
