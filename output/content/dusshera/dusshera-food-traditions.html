A Taste of Victory: Dussehra's Culinary Traditions

Dussehra is a celebration that delights the senses, blending vibrant rituals with diverse culinary traditions. Throughout India, the festival is celebrated with foods that signify festivity, break the Navaratri fast, and spread happiness. From flavorful snacks to indulgent sweets, join us in uncovering the delicious essence of Dussehra.

West India: Gujarat & Maharashtra

In Gujarat, the Dussehra morning tradition shines with the beloved pairing of Fafda and Jalebi.

  • Jalebi & Fafda: A timeless blend of flavors for breakfast. Fafda’s crunchy gram flour strips pair flawlessly with the sugary swirl of jalebis.
    Why: This dessert is a festive delight enjoyed after the Navaratri fast. Legend says Lord Rama cherished a sweet named 'Shashkuli,' akin to jalebi.
  • Puran Poli (Maharashtra): A delightful flatbread filled with a tasty blend of chana dal, jaggery, and hints of cardamom and nutmeg.
    Why: This classic celebratory dish is crafted during special events to symbolize joy and abundance.

East India: The Sweetness of Bengal

For Bengalis, Vijayadashami brings warm wishes ('Shubho Bijoya') and sharing of festive sweets.

  • Rasgulla & Sandesh: Bengali festivities feel incomplete without these classic sweets crafted from fresh chhena (cottage cheese), shared with guests to celebrate joyful new beginnings.
  • Luchi and Alur Dom: Puffy, golden-fried bread (luchi) paired with a rich, spiced potato curry (alur dom) is a classic celebratory dish.
    Why: Following the immersion of Goddess Durga's idol, families gather and exchange visits. These dishes play a key role in fostering hospitality and the camaraderie of Bijoya Dashami.

South India: Offerings of Golu

In South India, Navaratri is marked by 'Golu', a festive arrangement of dolls, with daily offerings of special 'prasad'.

  • Sundal: A flavorful, nutritious dish of boiled legumes—chickpeas, black-eyed peas, or peanuts—seasoned with mustard seeds, curry leaves, and fresh coconut.
    Why: Each of the nine nights features a unique sundal as prasad, offering protein and simplicity for those observing the fast.
  • Payasam: A South Indian dessert, kheer is a creamy pudding of rice, vermicelli, or lentils, simmered in milk or coconut milk and sweetened with sugar or jaggery.
    Why: A festive treat symbolizing the grand finale of Vijayadashami celebrations.

The Navaratri Fasting Feast

The nine days before Dussehra are marked by fasting (vrat) for many, featuring a traditional 'saatvik' diet.

  • Foods Avoided: Wheat, rice, lentils, onions, garlic, and meat are commonly excluded.
  • Popular Fasting Foods:
    • - Sabudana (Sago): Used to make khichdi, vada, and kheer.
    • - Kuttu (Buckwheat) & Singhara (Water Chestnut) Flour: Used to make puris, parathas, and halwa.
    • - Makhana (Fox Nuts): Roasted as a snack or used to make kheer.
    • - Fruits & Milk Products: Form a major part of the fasting diet.
    Why: This saatvik diet is seen as cleansing and pure, aiding in body detoxification and enhancing mental focus on spiritual practices during the sacred nine nights.
